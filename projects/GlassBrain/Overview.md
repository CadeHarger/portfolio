GlassBrain was a startup I created as a part of Trinity University's Stumberg Entrepreneurship program. I Designed a visual programming language for neural networks and platformed it with a full-stack desktop app. The core thesis of the startup was that:
- Neural Networks are better understood via visual flowcharts
- By making flowcharts interactive and heirarchal, they can become a full neural-network language
- Since this is more understandable, it's an easier place for people to learn from.

My plan was to monetize this by selling educational content integrated into the IDE as well as weekly paper implementations, helping people quickly understand new papers and new architectures. 

This visual programming language created a novel set of abstractions that simplified concepts like complex math, tensor shape, backpropagation, and training loops and instead focused on architecture design choices and building learner intuition. 

Frontend: React, Redux, React Flow
Backend: Python, Flask, PyTorch
Desktop app: Electron

As a part of this startup, I founded a Texas LLC and competed in the Stumberg pitch competition and won $5,000 of no-equity pre-seed funding in March of 2023. I built GlassBrain over the summer while participating in the Stumberg Summer Accelerator and Geekdom Incubator programs. That October, I competed for an additional $25,000 in funding. 

## What I learned:

I've grown so much as a developer since doing this. I taught myself javascript, React, Flask, and Electron all while doing this, so it's safe to say I learned a lot from that alone. This was my first time building a project with much size, so I learned a lot about the importance of elegant class structure, tests, and other scalability/reliability practices. For most of the summer, I was really just trying to get to the MVP, so I cut a lot of corners when developing (little test cases, bad backend abstractions) that would hurt if I tried to go back to it. I was also trying to design the neural network abstractions first, and then figure out how to port it to Pytorch. Having to do it all now, I'd probably rewrite it with AI coding, but if not, I'd just make a Pytorch visual editor, and then add my own abstractions slowly after seeing where there's opportunity. I'd probably come to similar conclusions for what should happen, but I think the backend would be structured much better. 

I had a ton of fun designing this; the abstractions were cool and I genuinely felt like I'd found a way to simplify things. It was always satisfying to just say "Scratch, but for neural networks" and instantly see any software person's eyes light up because they saw the value. I learned so much about startups and saw the real world for the first time. I remember at Geekdom someone offering me a drink but I had to decline as I was 20. The next youngest person in the room was 28, so nobody second guessed whether I could. I was surrounded by people who were risking a lot for their dreams, including a single mother who had quit her job to start a flower business. I am very grateful for the opportunity to participate in these programs and the unrelenting support I had.
